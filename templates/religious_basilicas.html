Basilicas

1. Arapaj Basilica (Durrës)

Arapaj Paleochristian Basilica represents one of the most important ecclesiastical complexes of late antiquity in Albania. It dates to the 5th–6th centuries AD and is associated with the consolidation phase of Christianity in the province of New Epirus. Its plan is triple-aisled, with three naves separated by columns or pilasters. The eastern apse is emphasized and designed according to the Roman basilical model adapted to Christian architecture. The building was part of a larger episcopal complex. The floor mosaics are among the most important artistic elements. Motifs are geometric, with interlaced compositions and Christian symbolism. The execution technique demonstrates a high level of craftsmanship. Construction materials include local limestone and reused Roman bricks. Stratigraphy suggests multiple rebuilding phases. Its proximity to Dyrrachium (ancient Durrës) indicates connections with the main urban center. The basilica demonstrates an organized ecclesiastical structure and may have had funerary functions in a later phase. Architecturally, it is monumental compared to rural basilicas. The interior was designed for liturgical processions. Columns created symbolic divisions of the sacred space. Mosaics served as a catechetical iconographic program. Today mainly foundations and flooring survive; the superstructure is completely lost. The Arapaj Basilica remains a primary reference for the study of paleo-Christian architecture in central Albania.

2. Basilica A — Byllis

Basilica A of Byllis is one of the most monumental buildings in the city during late antiquity. It dates to the 5th century AD. The structure is triple-aisled with a deeply set eastern apse. The naves are separated by regular colonnades. The floor is covered by polychrome mosaics. Motifs include geometric ornaments and Christian symbolism. The plan is clear and preserved at foundation level. The basilica was part of Byllis’s episcopal complex, and its position in the city underlines its central importance. The architecture shows early Byzantine influence. The building is constructed over earlier urban layers. Construction materials include local stone and reused Roman elements. The mosaics attest to a developed local craft tradition. The basilica represents Byllis’s transformation from an Illyrian town into a Christian center. The building hosted liturgical and administrative functions. Auxiliary spaces suggest advanced organization. Stratigraphy indicates repair phases after seismic events. Today foundations and large parts of the floors survive. The structure is among the most studied sites in Albania. Basilica A is a typical example of urban paleo-Christian architecture.

3. Basilica of Lumas

The Basilica of Lumas is one of the most important rural basilicas in central Albania. It dates to the 5th–6th centuries. The plan is triple-aisled with an eastern apse. The structure is mainly preserved at foundation level. Mosaics are the site’s most important element, featuring geometric motifs with regular compositions. The floor demonstrates refined technique with small tesserae. The architecture is simpler than urban examples. Its hilly location suggests a function for a local community. The basilica may have been the center of an organized rural area. Construction materials include local stone bonded with lime mortar. The structure lacks vertical superstructure. Stratigraphy suggests a single main phase of construction. The mosaics display early Byzantine symbolic elements. The building is evidence of Christianity’s spread into rural areas. The spatial organization is typical for provincial basilicas. The apse is pronounced and distinctive. The Basilica of Lumas represents rural paleo-Christian typology and is important for the study of Christianity in Myzeqe.

4. Great Basilica — Butrint

The Great Basilica of Butrint is among the most important early Christian monuments in the Balkans. It dates to the 6th century. The plan is triple-aisled with an atrium and auxiliary spaces. The apse is monumental and architecturally developed. The floor is covered with decorative mosaics. The iconographic program is complex. The architecture shows direct influence from Constantinople. The structure is part of an episcopal complex and was built over earlier Roman layers. Materials include reused Roman elements. The building served as an administrative and liturgical center. The space was designed for major ceremonial events. Columns created rhythmic divisions of the naves. The basilica represents the culmination of paleo-Christian development in Albania. Stratigraphy shows post-seismic rebuildings. The mosaics are among the best preserved in the region. The superstructure is partially preserved. The site is part of the UNESCO World Heritage. The basilica demonstrates Butrint’s integration into the Byzantine world and remains the most monumental paleo-Christian monument in Albania.

5. Basilica of Lin

The paleochristian Basilica of Lin dates to the 4th–5th centuries and is among the earliest in the Albanian territory. It is one of the earliest examples preserved in the area. The structure is mainly preserved in its mosaic floors. The plan is triple-aisled with an eastern apse. Mosaics present polychrome geometric motifs with a refined composition. The technique indicates direct Mediterranean influences. The site is located near Lake Ohrid, indicating links to lake-based trade routes. Materials are local stone. The superstructure is completely lost. Stratigraphy indicates a main primary phase. The basilica represents the early phase of ecclesiastical organization. Mosaics are among the most important finds in eastern Albania. The architecture is a provincial Roman adaptation to Christian liturgical needs. The site provides crucial data for early Christian archaeology in the region.
